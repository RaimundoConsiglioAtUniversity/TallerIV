Lost Little Horsies

A game design project by Raimundo Consiglio

About My Proposal

Lost Little Horsies is a non-violent¹, 2D platforming game where a group of three horsies —a Unicorn, a Pegasus, and a Gæahippos— pronounced /ˈge.ɪp.ɒs/ from Greek Γαῖα (Earth), and ίππος (Horse) try to find their way home after being lost in early 19th century Britain.

They journey through the land while working together, taking advantage of each of their unique abilities, avoiding magic hunters who want to keep them for their collections, and making friends along the way.

Rated E/E+10, for PC and consoles.

Inspiration - Concept

Lost Little Horsies was born out of my love for platforming games, as well as my passion for My Little Pony.

Inspiration - Character Swapping

The key mechanic of swapping between characters to complete platforming challenges came to me while playing Mario & Luigi: Superstar Saga, where you swap between the titular characters to navigate the world using their unique field abilities, which all have them cooperate to perform these.

My brain then made the connection that this could be used in platforming games, and it has been used in games like Sonic Heroes and The Lost Vikings. Beyond those two examples, I feel that this still is fertile grounds for exploration of the concept, since not many other games feature this as a core gameplay mechanic.

Inspiration - Non-Violence

Before having the character swapping mechanic pop into my mind, though, the concept initially came to my mind as a simple question: How would a platforming game where you don't inflict violence play like? Because, even if cartoony, games like Mario, Kirby, and Sonic feature some type of violence as core gameplay mechanics, be it Mario's stomp, Kirby's inhaling, or Sonic's spin dash. I though it could make for an interesting design challenge, and the character swapping mechanics came not long after.

These two ideas also perfectly fit a game inspired by My Little Pony, since core messages of the series include the importance of friendship and collaboration, as well as perseverance and understanding.

Inspiration - Plot

I needed a compelling narrative for the game, though, so I got thinking: What if three little ponies got stranded in the human world? After all, the idea of being stranded in a world that's not your own is quite common in anime, to the point that this trope has earned itself a name: Isekai, meaning "Other-World". Even classical western literature isn't exempt from this, a couple of examples being Alice in Wonderland and The Chronicles of Narnia. Many fanfics in the My Little Pony community feature humans being accidentally transported to Equestria as well!

Inspiration - Plot

The trope usually plays out with humans being stuck in fantasy worlds, but despite being less common, it can also play out in reverse; heck, a few of the aforementioned fanfics feature ponies being stranded in earth, so why not play with the trope myself? It gives our protagonists a sympathetic, yet easy to understand motive: Who hasn't felt alienated, strange sometimes when in a new environment, be it a new school, a new workplace, or a country your visiting?

Inspiration - Setting

With that established, I then had to come up with an appropriate setting.

I quickly came up with the concept of these ponies being stuck in a period loosely taking place in 18th century Britain, while taking elements from between the 16th and 19th century as well, since it could pose for an interesting juxtaposition with my characters. In a time of transition from magical thinking to staunch rationalism, what's better to upset the status quo than three mythical creatures popping up unannounced?

What is a Puzzle Platformer?

Lost Little Horsies is a 2D puzzle-platformer. In his blog, kelleherbros.com, video game designer Lucas Kelleher defines platformers as "game[s] where the primary action revolves around running and jumping." steoretipically, these are "2D side-scrolling game[s] in which the main character navigates horizontal space by running and vertical space with jumping," and then goes on to define puzzle-platformers as "platforming game[s] built around puzzle-solving, as opposed to combat/action or freewheeling traversal." Lucas then gives us Donkey Kong '94 as an example of such games, describing its gameplay as such: "[Mario's] objective is almost always to bring a key back to a locked door, so that he might proceed to the next challenge. It’s platforming gameplay, but always [as] a means to solve [a] puzzle", which I feel is a very apt description of what I plan to do with Lost Little Horsies.

Objetivo Principal

Diseñar un videojuego 2D del género puzzle-platformer que se desenlazaría en un mundo inspirado en la Gran Bretaña de inicios del siglo 19, donde el jugador o jugadora navega niveles cuyos entornos requieren de su pensamiento abstracto. Esto se lograría alternando entre 3 personajes, cada uno con habilidades únicas. La narrativa yuxtapondría el pensamiento mágico de los siglos anteriores —representado por los personajes mitológicos con un aspecto dulce y pueril— con el pensamiento científico traído por la reciente revolución científica y la revolución industrial, manteniendo una narrativa no-violenta con la intención de comunicar que uno no tiene por qué dejar atrás a su niño o niña interior.

Objetivos específicos

Diseñar un sistema de movimiento satisfactorio, donde controlar los tres personajes con distintas habilidades se sienta intuitivo. Estas habilidades estarían balanceadas de tal modo que ninguno de estos personajes se sientan innecesarios o redundantes. Los niveles del juego estarían diseñados de tal modo que el jugador o la jugadora pueda explorar y explotar las habilidades de cada personaje para avanzar.

Objetivos específicos

Crear un mundo cuyos aspectos narrativos y de jugabilidad yuxtapogan lo mágico y lo científico, usando este de vehículo narrativo para contar una historia que explore el concepto de que no hay que perder el asombro —nuestro niño o niña interior— para vivir en un mundo adulto.

Objetivos específicos

Desarrollar, implementar, y evaluar los aspectos visuales, sonoros, narrativos, y jugables del juego, de modo que estos fortalezcan tanto el diseño mecánico como narrativo de este.
